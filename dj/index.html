SETTING

Oh, it’s bad. Automation means that unless you’re an owner or lucky enough to have a job that requires actual human skills, you’re slumming it. Hard. So what do you do? You hustle. You scam. You steal. You doublecross. You make friends. And you try to stay one step ahead of everyone who’s trying to do the same to you.

But the second tech revolution isn’t without some benefit. Most everyone gets hardwired for net access real young—hospitals are even offering it at birth—artificial organs can keep you alive and even healthier than human, provided you can either make your interest payments or stay one step ahead of the repo man, and cybernetic enhancements are as common as tattoos and piercings, if not moreso.

The big corporate boys still run the show, but there's plenty of place in the middle, all those elevated gangsters living that petty bouj high life. Anywhere you look, somebody's trying to squeeze you out, shut you down, shut you up, wipe you out, co-opt you, break you down, pull one over on you. What can you say, it's a living.

You and your crew have managed to eke out a kind of anxious stability: you’ve got your turf, and you’ve done well enough to not be on anyone major’s radar. Until today that is, when somebody’s cousin limps into the door of the pizza joint, opium den, backroom casino, Hot Topic, community garden, tattoo parlor, underage bar, abandoned public school gymnasium, or wherever it is you and your crew call home. And they’ve got bad news—some bogeyman’s decided you’ve stuck your all’s head out just high enough from the human filth pile to be noticed. Time to put on your danger face.

THE DECK

Your deck is your life. It controls the actions and flows of the world. You’ll be drawing through your deck for combat and checks—anything that requires more than casual effort. Your deck starts off with 27 cards: one full suit each in red and black, and one joker. Jacks are worth 11, queens 12, kings 13, and aces 14 when they need to be a number. There are a number of keywords that will come up that you need to know:

  • Draw. Take however many cards from your deck, put them in your hand. "Drawing up" is drawing enough cards to get back to max hand size (usually 5).
  • Flip. Flip the top card of your deck over and discard it. The difficulty of the task determines what you'll need to flip to succeed: flip target is the hardest, then flip ace, then flip face, then flip black/red.
  • Discard. Remove a card from your hand or deck into a face-up pile.
  • Ace. Aces are crits—both fails and hits. They do different things depending on context.
  • Shuffle. Shuffle your hand, discards, and deck. You shuffle after a combat encounter, checks, when resting in combat, and when you draw a joker.
  • Joker. Drawing a joker activates your joker skill and has you shuffle and draw up on the spot.

The GM uses a full deck of 54 (both jokers) for his/her side of encounters—that means all 4 guys in that rival biker gang or whatever are drawing from the same deck. If you’re fighting areal tough dude though, one of those real legendary motherfuckers, the GM might use a separate 27-card deck just for that guy.

CHARACTERS

Characters in DECKJAMMERS have no class or race (but they are low-class and of any human race you'd like). Life for your character has been pretty shitty so far, and nobody expects it'll get much better. Except now, your back's right up against the wall—and who knows, you might even come out the other end a litter better off.

Your crew, made up of the characters you and your friends are playing as, is a big part of your character's identity. You've known each other for a while at least, probably, and you've got each others' backs. That's not to say you're all clones or something like that; far from it. Think of any crew from literally any movie, tv show, book, comic, whatever: you've each got your own personalities, strengths, and weaknesses, but hopefully you're all different enough to cover all the bases without any too-gaping holes. That's why you really should all do character creation together.

Character creation is done in three steps. 1: Get an idea for your character. What are they like? Actually take a minute to describe your character's personality, likes, dislikes, talents, aesthetic, all that, and give yourself some kind of defining characteristic: a quirk, shtick, signature item—an iconic element of self. Step 2 is figuring out your attributes (see below) and step 3 is buying your skills and enhancements.

Main attributes

There are five attributes: face, footwork, handeye, toughness, and smarts. Secondary attributes like HP and defensive stats are derived from these, and your attribute scores directly affect how well you can do the things you want to do. At character creation, you have 10 points to distribute among the five attributes, but you can’t put more than 4 points into one attribute.

Face is the social attribute. It’s the measurement of your charm, charisma, confidence, ability to successfully bullshit, negotiate, barter, inspire, and persuade.

Footwork is the agility attribute. More points means you’re faster and harder to hit with ranged attacks, like by bullets from guns.

Handeye is the dexterity attribute. Most of the time, this one is going to come into play when you’re trying to shoot things or people. Also, driving and playing videogames. And stealing.

Toughness is the strength attribute. It’s used for three major things: calculating your HP, how hard it is to damage you with a melee attack, and how well you can do melee attacks.

Smarts is the knowledge attribute. Need to remember something? Need to research something? Need to make repairs? Need to tell if someone’s lying? Need to hack some shit? Smarts is it.

Derived attributes

Your HP is 1 plus your toughness score. Damage in DECKJAMMERS is abstracted fairly heavily: if an attack hits you, you’re most likely taking one point of damage (unless their attack was a pretty dope skill, in which case it’ll probably be more than one). If your HP gets to 0, you’re KO’d and all you can do is flip one on your turn until you’re no longer KO’d. If your HP hits your negative toughness score, you’re dead. We’ll get to the rules for all that later though.

The order in which you’ll act in combat encounters is based on your footwork score. Depending on how your GM wants to do it, there’s two ways to handle initiative. The first is more dynamic but also there’s more to keep track of: everyone (including each bad guy) flips a card, adds their footwork score to it, and then you all go in descending order. In case of a tie, a black flipped card will always beat a red flipped card; if they’re both the same, work it out amongst yourselves. The second is you just go in order of highest total footwork for each side, and your crew and the bad guys acts in blocs.

How far you can move in combat is also determined from footwork. DECKJAMMERS uses squares, like on those battlemat grids. A square’s either 5ft or 2m a side, or somewhere in between, depending on your feelings about the metric system and how big of a rules wonk nob you are. Everyone gets 4 squares of movement, plus one more for every point they have in footwork. Yes, that does mean you can be very, very fast if you want to be.

Optional: Hobbies

If you'd like, you can choose a hobby for your character. Each hobby has a profiency level—novice, amateur, adept, expert—that increases as you use your hobby. Hobbies are designed to flesh out chars a bit and also (potentially) help out the group as a whole. They are not tied to a specific attribute.

Drugs. Drugs are great for big boosts up front but with drawbacks later. Having drugs as a hobby also gets expensive. There is also a push-your-luck aspect here with the chance to OD.

  • Novice/cornerkid: the pills you can get ($10/ea) give a +1 to any attribute for one encounter, but then a -1 to that same attribute for the next one.
  • Amateur/connoseur: you can now start mixing pills to get a +2 to any attribute for one encounter (with the same drawback as a cornerkid), but also flip every time you take (or administer) pills: red ace means you have a bad trip, and all your attributes in the next encouter are -1.
  • Adept/functional addict: you've got the chops to choose just the right chemicals. Your pills double in cost, efficacy, and in drawback. Flip: red face means you have a bad trip, red ace means you OD: you are immediately KO'd and you have the bad trip drawback in your next encounter.
  • Expert/pharmacologist: remove both Jacks from your deck. Your pills can no longer cause people to OD and you have to take pills every encounter.

Cooking. Cooking meals for your crew provides small but persistent buffs for the whole day, and is generally cheaper than drugs, though getting your hands on some of the higher-tier ingredients might run up quite the tab. You can make one meal per day.

  • Novice/cart: you know one recipe, and it's eggs and spam in ramen. It's good, sure, but there's not a whole lot to it. It costs $5 to make a pot for the crew, and everyone can choose one card in their deck and add 8 to its value until the end of the day.
  • Amateur/diner: you've figured out a couple more recipes. Your paninis ($3/ea) let a crewmember add 6 to two cards in their deck for the day and your brownies ($9/all) let everyone who eats them remove a red non-face card from their deck for the day.
  • Adept/restaurant: you've mastered some harder recipes and you've even developed a signature dish. Whatever that might be (it's on the level of fanciness of like a $18 cordon bleu) gives everyone that eats it the ability, once per day, when they'd have to flip any number of cards, to flip one extra one and choose the result they'd like.
  • Expert/michelin: you're good enough to win on Iron Chef for sure at this point. Everything you cook is now twice as fancy, meaning everything costs twice as much, but is twice as effective. Eggspamramen and brownies work on two cards, paninis work on 4 cards, and your signature dish lets people use that bonus flip ability twice in one day.

Scavenging. Scavenging parts will get you earn you some bonus cash on the side, and as you get better at it, you might even find some dope shit other people have left behind (like that spare liver that girl had hiding in her leg).

  • Novice/mouse: you have a knack for finding loose change. After every encounter flip: you find money equal to the value of the card you drew.
  • Amateur/racoon: when you scavenge, add 4 to the value of the card you flip. If you flipped face, you also find 4 rounds of ammo.
  • Adept/vulture: when you scavenge, add 8 to the value of the card you flip. If you flip black, you also find as many rounds of ammo as the value of the card. If you flip face, you harvest any cyber enhancements from defeated (hopefully dead, lol) enemies that you can sell for 1/4 of their cost.
  • Expert/jackal: when you scavenge, add 12 to the value of the card you flip. If you flip black, you also find 4+ as many rounds of ammo as the value of the card. If you flip face, you harvest any cyber enhancements from defeated (hopefully dead, lol) enemies that you can sell for 1/4 of their cost. If you flip ace, you find a hidden cache of goods worth at least $100 (though the specifics are determined by the GM).

Trolling. Trolling ups your crew's notoreity online and can even start to bring in some persistent income once you get enough fans, but it does come with the potential drawback that you'll attract some haters who might want to take it out on your IRL.

  • Novice/shitposter: during a combat encounter, when you take a rest, an enemy of your choice flips: if red face, they have to discard a card at random from their hand.
  • Amateur/memester: make a face check as an action in combat with a target of 20 against somebody. If you succeed, reduce your target's max hand size by 1. If you fail, they draw 2 cards and can take an immediate action against you.
  • Adept/shitlord: your reputation is starting to precede you. At the beginning of every encounter, flip. If red ace, your enemies call in some reinforcements and another of their crew joins the fight. If black ace or joker, turns out one of your enemies is a fan of your antics and refuses to fight you.
  • Expert/netceleb: your crew has a permanent 20% discount everywhere but every time you buy something, flip: if red face, you are chased out the store and can't buy anything until after your next encounter.

CREWS

While you inhabit your character, your character is inseparable from your crew, so figuring out your crew identity is important. Decide as a group your crew's name, home base, and what you're known for. Write down your connections to and relationships with other crews: allies, enemies, rivals, mentors, etc. Also come up with a handful of other people that support or are at least peripheral to your crew: Mama Chow that owns the noodle shop you're based out of, the friendly drug dealer Kid Koolzone, your motorcycle mechanic Sid Vicarious, BallDrape699 the master hacker, the crew's collective little brother Puzo, etc.

Come up with a style, a vibe, a credo, a raison d'être. Look at your characters in relation to your crew: who does what? what's your role in the crew?

You also need to figure out how your crew gets around. Without a sick ride, are you even a crew? The list below are guided examples rather than an exhaustive list.

You can also mod out your ride. There are cheap mods in the $100-$200 range that can give you a small boost, like a NOS kit that consumes a canister to double your speed for a round or a directional satellite dish that double the range of your electronics. There are more expensive mods too that add much better bonuses, but they also add some bummers. Negotiate with your GM roleplaying a chopshop mechanic to figure out the deets.

On bonuses and bummers: these are narrative tags that you and the GM decide what they mean when you write them down on your list of shit your crew has. Even then though, be loose about it. Your rides shouldn't be overpowered dei ex machina, but useful tools. So what does "traveling arsenal" mean? It's already pretty descriptive, figure it out on you own, chumhead.

Crew Cash

The other big thing your crew needs to keep track of is money. Specifically in 2 separate ways:

  1. The total amount of money your crew has generated. Acts as a gauge for your crew's notoreity and the abilities of its members. When your crew has an amount set by the GM, say like $10k, your joker skills level up.
  2. The current balance of the crew's account and a note of impending payments at the end of the month. Knowing this information is a good propellant for the types of missions you might want to undertake, and when the extremely scary people you owe money to will come after you.

SHOPLISTS

There is no XP in DECKJAMMERS. Instead, you take the cash money you get for completing assignments, beating fools, legitimate business enterprises (lol), and outright theft and you turn that into new and better gear, and new and better skills (which are in the next section). Nevertheless, your crew or the GM should keep a running total of money earned as a general gauge for your crew’s progress.

At character creation, you’ll start out with $473 each—it’s all you were able to scrounge together, divided by how many of you there are in your crew. It’s not much, but it’s enough to get started. Especially in a world where you can bet just about everyone is kitted out in some way or another. In other words: unless you buy some cool-ass fists, your punches ain't doing shit to nobody. With $473 you won’t be able to afford any of the highest-tech gear and cybernetics of course, but that money’s good at Crazy Hakeem’s Discount Gun Emporium and Dok Roberks’ Bakkalley Kcybers, which are as good as places as any to get started. Oh, and there’s always the more legitimate outfits as well, like Lexwell Cybernetics, Inc., but given your abysmal credit history, the interest rate on the loan they give you is going to be killer. Probably literally if you miss a payment. But seriously, your interest rate is 31.4%.

Cybernetics are limited by your body. Meaning, you have two eyes, so unless you shell out for a third, you can only take two eye skills. If some cyber part replaces, for example, both legs, then you can’t take another leg enhancement. Use common sense here.

Some of these give you a new attack--jot that down on your sheet somewhere, you can use those like you would a melee weapon or gun or something. Others might tell you you have a new skill, in which case you'd add a skill you bought outright (again, covered in the next section).

You might notice there’s no armor. Duh. Who the hell wears armor in an urban proletarian hellscape? This isn’t Mad Max.

SKILLS

You purchase skills like you would gear. When purchasing skills, you have two options. First, you can buy a new skill, which you then assign to a particular card: suit and number, like 4♣. Second, you can improve your expertise at a skill, where you then assign that skill to another card. Doing this though costs 50% more than the previous time you bought that skill. Because the value of the card is replaced by a skill, it’s a smart move to start assigning skills at the 2s and move upwards from there.

Unlike gear, skills come in tiers, grouped by cost. At higher tiers, the skills will be a combination of more interesting, better, more niche, having prerequisites, and so on.

Oh, you also get to start with a joker skill. These are bigger and splashier than other skills. After you play a joker in combat, take it out of your deck until the end of the combat encounter. They're also more archetypal, a signature ultimate action. They should in some way be tied to your character's cybernetic enhancements, favored weapon, or at the very least your highest, bestest attribute. Below are some examples, but feel free to make up one that works for you, your crew, and your GM. Can’t be too overpowered, right? You choose one at character creation, [and it gets stronger at regular intervals, which is why it’s important to keep track of the crew’s gross earnings.]

Some of the skills look like they’re items. That’s because they are. Items are special once-per-encounter skills that you take out of your deck instead of into your discard after you play them, like with joker skills.

RULES

Checks

Your GM will ask you to make a check if you’re trying to do something that requires more than a normal amount of skill or expertise. Normal movement, talking, reading—all that stuff just happens. But let’s say you’re trying to jump across to the next building, convince an NPC to do something, hold a door shut, hack into a system, pickpocket a fool—time to make a check.

To make a check, flip, from a freshly shuffled deck, cards equal to your the score in the relevant attribute. Look back up at what attributes cover and make the best choice here. If what you’re trying to do saddles the line between a couple attributes, you can argue your case and how you’ll try to accomplish what you’re going for to the GM. If he/she finds that reasonable, go for it.

From those cards you flipped, choose the two highest values, add them up, and add your attribute score to that. Tell the GM the total. For a relatively doable task, you’re looking to hit a 13. Harder tasks scale up from there to 30, which is an almost-impossible task. If it’s a task someone can reasonably help you with (meaning they have at least 3 in that attribute), they help you by letting you draw an extra card.

Here are the exceptions and caveats and special rules. Aces here work differently: a black ace means you caught a break so all your black cards are worth +2 (which also means that ace is worth 14+2=16); flipping a red ace means something messed up and all red cards you flipped are worth just 2. If you flip a joker, you can either replace one of the cards you flipped or flip an additional card. If one of the cards you flip is a skill, instead of choosing two cards to use on the check, you can instead choose one and double its value.

Sometimes things happen to you and you have to react. For example, someone might be trying to sneak up on you, or you might have walked into a trap, or what you’re doing might trigger a silent alarm. In these situations, you’re making a reaction check. The GM will tell you to flip a card in reaction and will tell you the attribute you’re reacting with (like if your crew is walking through an alley and into a net trap nobody saw, footwork would help you jump out of the way). Refer to this chart to the right to see what you need to flip to succeed. Unlike in proactive checks, flipping aces in reactive checks do not crit.

You always shuffle after a skill check.

Example: Checks

R4mb0 wants to defuse a bomb he found under his motorcycle. He’ll need to use smarts to figure out which wire to cut. He’s got 3 in smarts.

The GM decides this is a pretty shitty homemade bomb, so R4mb0’s target is 18: tough, but not impossible.

R4mb0 flips 3 cards: 6♥, 2♥, 4♥. Not great, but he slotted the Heavy Smash skill into 2♥. That means he can double the value of a card, but 12 isn’t good enough. The bomb explodes.

The GM decides that he can make a reaction check. His 4 points in footwork means he needs to flip black.

R4mb0 flips 9♠. He’s able to jump away in time, but his bike’s totally trashed.

Combat encounters

Before a combat encounter gets under way, the GM will first give your crew a layout of the area, including buildings, objects usable for cover, and where the bad guys are that are in your crew’s line of sight (or that have otherwise given themselves away). You also figure out initiative order, which is described in the attributes section.

At the beginning of your turn in an encounter, you draw up to your max hand size, which starts at 5 cards. On your turn, you can do as much as little as you want, provided you have the cards.

If you want to move, discard a card from your hand to move up to your max (see attributes). Standing up from being knocked down counts as a move as well.

The most common basic actions in combat are melee and ranged attacks, but skills, reloading, and item use are also considered actions. Any actions not explicitly listed here, like grappling, dragging, etc., are still actions. All actions will require you to play 2 cards. For melee and ranged attacks, choose two cards, add their values, and add your relevant attribute score—for melee that’s generally toughness, for ranged that’s generally handeye (your gear or cyberware will tell you if you’ve got more or different options). Your attack hits if your total action value is higher than 13 + target’s relevant attribute (footwork to avoid a ranged attack, toughness to deflect a melee attack).

Ranged attacks and some actions given by cybernetics list their effective range in squares. This does not mean they are worthless outside of those ranges, but for every 1sq beyond the range, add 2 to the target score needed for a hit.

To reload, make sure you have ammo available, then discard any two cards in your hand. To use an item, discard another card and remove the item card from your deck until the end of the encounter. For skills, you play the skill card with another card in your hand. Your total action value is 2x the value of the second card plus your relevant attribute score.

Repairing stuff uses your smarts attribute. If something is damaged, it takes one turn to repair during combat. If something is broken, it can't be repaired during combat but can be with time outside of combat.

Aces count as 14s—the highest value card—and if played with a face card or a skill card, you critically hit, doing +1 damage and forcing the target to discard a card. Critical hits can be defended, but you can’t escape unscathed: you still take 1 damage total if you defend.

Playing a joker activates your joker skill. After your joker skill resolves, remove the joker from your deck until the end of the encounter and shuffle.

You (well, everyone) can also defend against attacks. To defend, you need to have cards in your hand—if you played them all on your turn, you can’t defend (remember, you only draw at the beginning of your turn). To defend an attack, you discard a card from your hand, but by doing so you reduce your max hand size by 1 until you rest.

Resting is a special action. You have to rest if you have no cards in your deck at the beginning of your turn, but you can also choose to rest on your turn. Resting takes your entire turn—you can’t attack then rest, or move then rest—but it lets you shuffle. You don’t regain any HP or anything though. Remember, you only draw your hand at the beginning of your turns, which means you don’t draw after you rest until it’s your turn again.

HPs, getting KO’d, and dying are all covered in the attribute section. One note though: if you are KO’d and need to flip but you have no deck, you shuffle then flip.

Disarming does what it says on the box: the target of a disarming attack drops their weapon, and sometimes it gets flung away. When someone gets disarmed, they flip: red face, the weapon is damaged (broken if red ace); otherwise, no problems.

You always shuffle after a combat encounter.

Example: combat encounters

R4mb0 and Devochkill are confronted in an alley by two members of the Country Club Boys. It’s a kind of wide alley, but there’s a large dumpster near our heroes and a fire escape near the CCBs on the western building. There’s trash everywhere and a couple rats skitter away.

The CCBs flip a J♣ (+3 footwork) and win initiative, then it’s Devochkill (flipped 8♥+3) and R4mb0 (flipped 4♠+4). The GM draws hands for the CCBs, and the players draw for themselves.

Round 1: One of the CCBs, let’s call him Don, discards 1 to climb up onto the fire escape. He takes a shot at Devochkill, playing K♦+9♣+3=25. Devochkill’s only got a toughness of 2 and hates getting chumped on the first round, so she discards a card to defend, avoiding the shot.

The other CCB, Ald, charges at R4mb0 with his baseball bat. He discards 1 to run up, then plays 9♦+10♥+3=22. That’s more than R4mb0’s 18 (13+5 toughness), so he takes 1 damage, putting him at 5/6 HP.

Because Devochkill defended Don’s attack, she can’t dive behind the dumpster like she wanted, so she just stays put and shoots at Ald with her shotgun, playing 5♥+J♠+4=20, dealing 1 damage. She then uses her 3♥ skill—Eye Laser: Superman Style—with a 7 for a total of 2x7+4=18, again dealing damage to Ald.

R4mb0 knows that Ald’s hurting bad, so he plays A♥+Q♥+5 to smash him with his hammer, dealing a critical hit. Ald has to defend or he’d be KO’d, so he discards a card, only taking 1 damage. R4mb0’s pissed that Ald defended, so he attacks again: J♠+7♥+5=23. Again, Ald has to defend, discarding his last card. With his last card, R4mb0 discards to duck behind the dumpster.

Round 2: Don draws back up to 5. From his vantage point, he’s going to take 2 shots at Devochkill since he can’t see R4mb0 anymore. He plays 6♣+Q♠+3=21 and 10♠+10♦+3=23. Devochkill can take them both and she will because she doesn’t want to be at further card disadvantage.

Ald can only draw 3 cards since he defended twice. Knowing he’ll probably die next turn, he’ll discard one to move up to Devochkill—maybe he can take her with him. He plays 8♣+K♠+3=24. Devochkill is pissed she has to defend again so she won’t get KO’d by this dick, so she does.

Devochkill can only draw up to 3 cards, but she smiles when she sees what she drew. She plays 2♥, which is her grenade, with a 10♠, totalling 10x2+4=24, lobbing it at Don. Don knows he’s screwed because the only way he can dodge the blast damage from the grenade is to jump off of the fire escape, but he does it anyway, discarding. Because he fell 2sq though, he takes 1 damage. The GM figures that he’ll have to evade the falling fire escape as well, so she has Don make a reaction check. His footwork is 3, so all he has to flip is black, but he flips 8♥. He takes another damage, is disarmed, knocked down, and pinned.

R4mb0 knows Ald has no cards left, so he draws up and plays K♠+6♥+5=24 to cave in Ald’s skull. He discards 1 to move up to Don. He sticks his sledgehammer in Don’s face then asks him if he’d rather die right here, right now, or if he’d like to explain why these fancy boys were so far out of their territory. Don says he’ll talk.

Wrap-up: Don and Ald had $57 on them, plus an SMG and a baseball bat. Don tells your crew that their leader, Green Jacket, is looking to expand his territory and was putting out feelers for easy pickings.

Other rules

Falling damage: 1 per every 2sq you fall straight down (this is assuming you’ve failed a reaction check or intentionally chose to fall or jump)

Switching weapons is a free action, meaning you can just do it. If you pick up a foe’s gun, flip for black to see if it’s loaded. The value of the black card you flip is how many rounds are loaded, up to the gun’s max.

Driving checks, both proactive and reactive, are going to be handeye. The difficulty of succeeding on a check should consider the quality of your vehicle, your familiarity with it, driving and road conditions, and what you’re trying to accomplish. Again, normal driving is a normal thing and doesn’t need a check.

If you flip a joker, your joker skill does not activate. You’ll succeed at whatever it was you flipped for no doubt, but the joker skips your hand and goes straight to the discard pile.

Yes, you can try to harvest cybernetics from fallen enemies. It is very hard to do it yourself, but you can also pay one of Dok Roberks’ interns to do it for you. To do it yourself, you need to succeed on both a smarts and a handeye check. It’s delicate work so nobody can help you out. The targets for those checks are at the GM’s discretion and should take into consideration your familiarity with cybernetics, that particular part/part of the body, and any studying you’ve done on the subject. 25 is about right though for a precision job.

Yes, you can try to implant cybernetics you’ve harvested or found by yourself. Again, it’s hard to do, but you can keep shelling out to those interns if you need to. To do it yourself to another crew member, you need to succeed on both a smarts and a handeye check. The same stipulations and caveats from harvesting apply here too.

Selling stuff you don’t want, like weapons and cybernetics, will give you 50% of its cost on the black market if it’s in good condition. 25% if it’s damaged. Use normal rounding rules, nobody deals in parts of dollars anymore.

It’s up to the GM to decide how time works, especially like when it’s been a month. A general rule of thumb though is that you and your crew can knock out one big job—a heist, an assassination, a recon, a shipment delivery, a meetup with a new contact—every week.

CHEATSHEET

Character creation

Put 10 points into the 5 attributes. No more than 4 in one, you can put 0.

  • Face: intimidate, charm, deal
  • Footwork: speed, dodging
  • Handeye: shooting, driving, stealing
  • Toughness: HP, blocking, melee
  • Smarts: hacking, repairing
  •  
  • HP = 1 + toughness
  • Movement = 4sq + footwork

Figure out what your crew's about and keep track of its lifetime earned cash and current balance and debts.

You have $473 to spend on gear and skills. You get 1 joker skill for free.

Gear usually gives you passive bonuses and determines your attacks and actions. They go on your character sheet when you buy them.

Skills generally modify your attacks and other actions. When you buy them, you assign them to a card value.

Flip

Most checks are resolved by flipping cards. Checks can be proactive (player-initiated) or reactive (GM-initiated).

Proactive: flip X, where X is the number of points in the relevant attribute. Add the two highest values and your attribute score. If the total meets or exceeds the target, you succeed. A target of 13 requires a bit of effort, 30 is almost impossible. The GM determines the target before you flip. If you flip a black ace, all your black cards are +2. If you flip a red ace, the value of all your red cards are 2. If you flip a joker you can either replace a card or flip an additional card. If you flip a skill card, choose one card you flipped and double its value.

Reactive: flip 1. Your attribute score determines the target you need to flip: 5+ needs ≥5, 3-4 needs black, 1-2 needs face or ace, 0 needs ace. Flipping a joker is an automatic success. Aces have no additional effect, nor do skill cards.

Combat

Initiative order: flip 1 + footwork.

Starting hand size is 5, draw up to it.

  • Movement: discard a card, move up to your max
  • Melee attack: add values of 2 cards + toughness*, target = 13 + opponent's toughness
  • Ranged attack: add values of 2 cards + handeye*, target = 13 + opponent's footwork
  • Reload: discard two cards
  • Skills: double value of one card + relevent attribute for the action (skills that are items or do not modify an attack must still be played with a second card)
  • Joker: activates your joker skill, then remove this card from your deck and reshuffle
  • Repair: add values of 2 cards + smarts, target determined by GM depending on how damaged the item is
  • Defend: discard a card and reduced your hand size by 1
  • Rest: discard as many cards as you like and reshuffle (you have to rest if your deck is empty, and you cannot take any other actions on turns when you rest)

* some gear or skills might tell you to use a different attribute
